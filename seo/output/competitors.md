## The competitive landscape splits in two

### A. Dispatch services (techrig's current base)
- **Direct peers:** maxdispatchservice.com, logitydispatch.com. Both run trailer-type dispatch silos like techrig but rank far better. maxdispatch reaches page 1 on ~23 terms despite a junk backlink profile — meaning on-page relevance + content, not authority, is winning these mid-volume terms. **Copyable:** their silo coverage and FAQ structure. **Their weakness:** generic, undifferentiated copy.
- **SERP ceiling (not competitors, do not try to beat for info terms):** dat.com, truckstop.com (load boards), thetruckersreport.com (forum, 81k keywords), freightwaves.com (news). These own informational dispatch queries. Compete with them only via commercial-intent pages and genuinely original content, not "what is" articles.

### B. Compliance / setup (the pivot) — SERP is wide open commercially
Flagship SERP analyzed: "how to get mc and dot number" (techrig ranks pos 49 on 14k impressions). Page 1 is:
- **Government:** fmcsa.dot.gov (x2), txdmv.gov — own the definitional answers.
- **SERP features:** AI Overview, People Also Ask (4 questions), a video carousel (YouTube: Truckers Authority, CarrierForge), and a Discussions/Forums block (Reddit, TruckersReport, Facebook).
- **Commercial players that DO rank:** jjkeller.com (USDOT/MC Number Service — enterprise, dry), authorityexpressllc.com (small-business authority service — the closest peer), loves.com (Love's blog). In People Also Ask: logrock.com won a PAA with a clean 2026 cost-breakdown table; truckopedia.com won one with a direct "how long does it take" answer.
- **Read:** no single commercial brand dominates compliance the way DAT dominates dispatch. The space is gov + forums + a few mid-tier services. A focused, genuinely helpful, well-structured commercial site can take PAA boxes and long-tail commercial terms. The winning content format is explicit: cost tables, exact timelines, step-by-step, FAQ — answer the question better and more specifically than a .gov page does.

## What is copyable vs not
**Copyable (table stakes):**
- Trailer-type dispatch silo coverage (peers have it; techrig already does too).
- Compliance content formats that win features: fee/cost tables, "how long does it take" timelines, step-by-step authority guides, strong FAQ/PAA targeting.
- Clear pricing display (techrig already publishes BOC-3 $100, etc. — most competitors hide pricing; this is an advantage to lean into).

**Not copyable — techrig's actual moat:**
- **The integrated funnel.** authorityexpressllc and jjkeller do compliance; logity and maxdispatch do dispatch. **No competitor in this set does both** — file your authority AND keep your truck loaded afterward. "We set up your trucking company, then dispatch it for years" is a positioning no single competitor can claim. This is the thesis the whole revamp should sell.
- **Transparent fixed pricing** on compliance filings, paired with a no-contract / no-forced-dispatch retainer.
- **Real client setup-to-road stories** (pending from the business, `experience-notes.md`) — proof neither the enterprise brands nor the thin services offer.

## Strategy implications (feed Phases 6-9)
1. Compete on compliance via **structured, owner-operator-friendly content** that out-specifics .gov and out-helps J.J. Keller's dry enterprise pages. Target PAA questions and commercial long-tail, not head "what is" terms.
2. On dispatch, **deepen the box truck franchise** (where techrig already has relative strength) rather than spreading thin across all trailer types equally.
3. Lead every money page with the **setup → dispatch funnel** thesis; it is the one thing no competitor can copy.
4. Authority is low (AS 10): prioritize **winnable long-tail and content quality**; treat link-building as a parallel long-game, not a launch dependency.
5. Lean on **transparent pricing** as a differentiator (competitors hide it).
