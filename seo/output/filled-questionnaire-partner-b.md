Section 9 — Anything else

* A contrarian take, lesson, or story from your client work you’d be happy to see in a blog post or on a page:

Submitting an MC or USDOT application does not mean a trucking company is ready to operate. New carriers can reach the end of the 21-day protest period and discover that their authority still cannot activate because the BOC-3, insurance filing, UCR, or another compliance requirement was not completed correctly.

Choosing the wrong authority classification can be just as serious as leaving a filing incomplete. One Tech Rig client mistakenly registered as a freight forwarder when the company actually intended to transport freight using its own box truck. Tech Rig corrected the authority type, completed the BOC-3 and UCR, and the properly classified motor-carrier authority activated after the mandatory 21-day protest period.

Owner-operators are still drivers for Driver Qualification purposes. A carrier does not avoid the DQ-file requirement simply because the owner is the only person driving the truck.

Driver drug-testing requirements are also time-sensitive. In Felix’s case, an earlier drug test was more than 30 days old, so a new pre-employment test had to be arranged before the driver could satisfy the applicable pre-employment requirement.

Small mistakes in FMCSA records can create major operational problems. Felix omitted his apartment number when filing his registration, and his insurer warned that the policy could be cancelled unless the FMCSA record was corrected. Tech Rig submitted the MCS-150 correction, and the correct address appeared on the record within 24 hours.

The transition from older FMCSA systems to MOTUS has created another source of delays. Some records must be claimed, linked, or corrected before certain filings can be completed. Tech Rig’s role is not simply to submit forms, but to identify what is actually blocking a carrier and guide the client through the correct compliance sequence.
