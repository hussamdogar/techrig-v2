Before you file: decisions that shape everything

What will you haul, and where?

Cargo type and interstate vs intrastate decide which authority and filings you need.

What equipment?

Box truck, hotshot, reefer, flatbed, dry van, or power only. This affects insurance and, later, how you get loads.

Carrier or broker?

They are different authorities with different requirements.

A wrong call here is the single most expensive mistake we fix.

One carrier registered as a freight forwarder when he meant to haul his own freight, which did not authorize him to operate, and it cost him a refile and weeks of delay.

How to start a trucking company in 9 steps

Each step links to the exact filing when you are ready to act.

  1. MI 01 You are here

    Form your business entity

    Most carriers set up an LLC and get an EIN. This separates your business and personal liability.

  2. MI 02

    Get your USDOT number

    Your federal ID as a carrier. See USDOT registration.

  3. MI 03

    Get your MC operating authority

    If you will haul regulated freight for hire. See MC authority.

  4. MI 04

    File your BOC-3

    Names a process agent in every state; required before authority activates. See BOC-3 filing.

  5. MI 05

    Get insurance and have it filed with FMCSA

    Your authority will not activate until the insurance filing is in. See insurance filing.

  6. MI 06

    Register for UCR

    Annual, based on fleet size. See UCR registration.

  7. MI 07

    Set up driver compliance

    Driver qualification files, Clearinghouse, and a drug and alcohol consortium with pre-employment testing. This applies even if you are the only driver.

  8. MI 08

    Handle interstate operations filings

    IRP apportioned plates and IFTA if you run across state lines, plus an ELD.

  9. MI 09

    Get through the 21-day protest period and your first safety audit

    Authority can activate after the mandatory protest period; new carriers face a safety audit early, so your driver files and records need to be in order.

The mistakes that stall new carriers

  • Submitting an application is not the same as being ready to operate. Carriers reach day 21 and cannot activate because the BOC-3, insurance, or UCR was missing or wrong.
  • The wrong authority classification blocks the work you intended to do.
  • Owner-operators still need driver qualification files. Being the only driver does not waive it.
  • Drug tests expire. A pre-employment test older than 30 days can mean a new one before a driver can start.
  • Small record errors cause big problems. A missing apartment number on an FMCSA record nearly got a carrier's insurance cancelled.
  • The MOTUS transition adds delays. Records often must be claimed or linked before a filing will go through.

How to start a trucking company FAQ

How long does it take to start a trucking company?
The filings can be done quickly, but FMCSA requires a mandatory 21-day protest period before authority activates, and processing time varies. We do not promise a date.
Do I need an LLC to start a trucking company?
Not legally required, but most carriers form one to separate business and personal liability and to get an EIN.
Do I need both a USDOT and an MC number?
Many interstate for-hire carriers need both. Some carriers need only a USDOT number. It depends on your operation.
Can I do this myself?
Yes. Most of it is doable. People come to us when classifications, MOTUS, or the order of filings trips them up, or when they would rather not risk a delay.
What is the first thing I should do?
Decide what and where you will haul and your equipment, then form your entity. Those choices drive every filing after.
