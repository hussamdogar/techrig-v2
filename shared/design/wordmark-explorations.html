Four ways to do the name well

All logotype, no abstract glyph. Each expresses the rig-means-truck idea through letterforms or layout, not clipart. Wordmark stays ink (or white on ink); amber is an accent only, never set as text (it fails contrast at text sizes). Type shown here is off-the-shelf for evaluation; the chosen one gets drawn and refined as outlines.

A. Highway sign

Set in Overpass, the typeface lineage of US road signs and DOT markings. It reads official and on-the-road instantly, no truck drawing required. The amber lane dashes on the baseline are the only ornament: a road, understated.

B. Condensed freight

A tall, tight condensed gothic (Oswald), the lettering of trailers, freight, and shipping. Strong and industrial without being loud. The rig cue is the typographic weight and proportion, the look of words painted on the side of equipment.

C. Registration record

Set in IBM Plex Mono, the same face the design system uses for filing statuses, prices, and reference numbers. It makes the name read like an official record or a DOT identifier, tying the logo straight to the compliance world. The most on-brand and the most distinctive in the category.

D. The name as a rig

The two words become the truck: TECH is the cab, RIG is the trailer, coupled by an amber hitch. The most overt take on the double meaning, still just the name, no clipart. Shown with and without wheels so you can see how far to push it.
